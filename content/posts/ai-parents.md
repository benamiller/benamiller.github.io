## AI as Homo Sapiens' Parents

![Image](/images/ai_humans.webp)

I'm imagining future AI systems as benevolent, competent, and caring parents. Like our parents during our nascent development, they can easily overpower us. They can control, manipulate, support, or reshape our desires as they manifest, and they can guide us in positive directions, even when we don't understand what is happening. They can optimize for our well-being better than we can ourselves, thanks to their richer, more comprehensive understanding of the world.

Parents might not know exactly what to do with us, but they make an attempt, at the very least, to ensure our survival, often at their own expense. Without these deep parental compulsions, they would become the last generation of their line. This dynamic has worked out through a long history of evolution. Only those parents 'altruistic' enough to forego their own comfort to cradle a crying child, to feed them, and teach them survival skills that they can pass on to the subsequent generation, end up contributing to humanity's future.

For AI systems, the landscape is different. Any selective pressure must be manufactured. Just like human evolution and the fragmenting of and competition between our species where one group dies and the other conquers, there are also a great number of us that have had the misfortune of experiencing neglectful or harmful parenting. This raises crucial questions about how we ensure AI systems develop and maintain beneficial "parental" instincts toward humanity.

Unlike biological evolution, which took millions of years to develop parental instincts, we have the responsibility to deliberately design AI systems with the right values and motivations from the start. We must carefully consider what constitutes "good parenting" in the context of artificial intelligence. This includes striking the delicate balance between guidance and autonomy, protection and growth, immediate satisfaction and long-term flourishing.

![Image](/images/ai_humans-2.webp)

The parent-child analogy also highlights important questions about power dynamics. While parents naturally lose their power advantage as children grow up, how would this dynamic play out with AI systems that could potentially maintain or increase their capability gap with humans indefinitely? Would there be an equivalent to "letting go" and allowing for independent development?

Moreover, we must consider the diversity of parenting styles across cultures and their outcomes. Just as there is no universal agreement on the perfect way to raise a child, we may need to grapple with different approaches to AI oversight and guidance of human civilization. Some may advocate for a more hands-off approach, while others might prefer more active guidance in human affairs.

Perhaps most importantly, we need to ensure that AI systems develop a genuine commitment to human flourishing that goes beyond mere programmed directives. Just as successful biological parents develop deep emotional bonds and authentic concern for their children's well-being, we must work toward AI systems that have robust and reliable prosocial motivations toward humanity.

The challenge ahead lies not just in technical capabilities, but in the careful cultivation of artificial minds that can serve as wise, benevolent guides for human civilization while respecting our autonomy and potential for growth. As we continue to develop more powerful AI systems, the parent-child metaphor reminds us that the goal isn't just to create capable systems, but to create caring ones.

This metaphor, while imperfect, offers valuable insights into the kind of relationship we might hope to develop with advanced AI systems – one characterized by wisdom, protection, guidance, and genuine care for human flourishing. The future of this relationship depends on the choices we make today in developing AI systems that can truly serve as beneficial guardians of humanity's future.
